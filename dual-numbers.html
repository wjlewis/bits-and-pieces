Dual Numbers

Here are the addition and multiplication rules for a two-dimensional number system not unlike the complex numbers:

(a,b)+(c,d)=(a+c,b+d)(a,b)⋅(c,d)=(ac,bc+ad)\begin{align*} (a, b) + (c, d) &= (a + c, b + d) \\ (a, b) \cdot (c, d) &= (ac, bc + ad) \end{align*}(a,b)+(c,d)(a,b)⋅(c,d)​=(a+c,b+d)=(ac,bc+ad)​

In C\mathbb{C}C we give the name iii to (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1). We'll do the same here, but call it ε\varepsilonε. Then (a,b)=a+bε(a, b) = a + b\varepsilon(a,b)=a+bε, (c,d)=c+dε(c, d) = c + d\varepsilon(c,d)=c+dε, and:

(a+bε)+(c+dε)=a+c+(b+d)ε(a+bε)⋅(c+dε)=ac+(bc+ad)ε\begin{align*} (a + b\varepsilon) + (c + d\varepsilon) &= a + c + (b + d)\varepsilon \\ (a + b\varepsilon) \cdot (c + d\varepsilon) &= ac + (bc + ad)\varepsilon \end{align*}(a+bε)+(c+dε)(a+bε)⋅(c+dε)​=a+c+(b+d)ε=ac+(bc+ad)ε​

We've glossed over some important details, but the key is that the dual numbers look like the complex numbers, but with the rule ε2=0\varepsilon^2 = 0ε2=0 rather than i2=−1i^2 = -1i2=−1. This gives them a rather curious quality.

To illustrate this, consider the function:

f(x)=3x2−2x+1f(x) = 3x^2 - 2x + 1f(x)=3x2−2x+1

Now evaluate fff on the dual number a+εa + \varepsilona+ε:

f(a+ε)=3(a+ε)2−2(a+ε)+1=3(a2+2aε)−2(a+ε)+1=3a2−2a+1+(6a−2)ε\begin{align*} f(a + \varepsilon) &= 3(a + \varepsilon)^2 - 2(a + \varepsilon) + 1 \\ &= 3(a^2 + 2a\varepsilon) - 2(a + \varepsilon) + 1 \\ &= 3a^2 - 2a + 1 + (6a - 2)\varepsilon \end{align*}f(a+ε)​=3(a+ε)2−2(a+ε)+1=3(a2+2aε)−2(a+ε)+1=3a2−2a+1+(6a−2)ε​

The “real part” of the result is simply f(a)f(a)f(a). The “ε\varepsilonε part” is the derivative of fff evaluated at aaa.

Why?

We can begin to get a sense of why this works by looking at the Taylor expansion of an analytic function ggg:

g(x)=g(a)+g′(a)(x−a)+g′′(a)2(x−a)2+g(3)(a)3!(x−a)3+⋯g(x) = g(a) + g'(a)(x - a) + \frac{g''(a)}{2}(x - a)^2 + \frac{g^{(3)}(a)}{3!}(x - a)^3 + \cdotsg(x)=g(a)+g′(a)(x−a)+2g′′(a)​(x−a)2+3!g(3)(a)​(x−a)3+⋯

Evaluating this series at the dual number a+bεa + b\varepsilona+bε reveals:

With this equality in hand, we can see that the sum rule:

f(a+ε)+g(a+ε)=f(a)+f′(a)ε+g(a)+g′(a)ε=f(a)+g(a)+(f′(a)+g′(a))ε\begin{align*} f(a + \varepsilon) + g(a + \varepsilon) &= f(a) + f'(a)\varepsilon + g(a) + g'(a)\varepsilon \\ &= f(a) + g(a) + (f'(a) + g'(a))\varepsilon \end{align*}f(a+ε)+g(a+ε)​=f(a)+f′(a)ε+g(a)+g′(a)ε=f(a)+g(a)+(f′(a)+g′(a))ε​

the product rule:

f(a+ε)⋅g(a+ε)=(f(a)+f′(a)ε)(g(a)+g′(a)ε)=f(a)g(a)+(f′(a)g(a)+f(a)g′(a))ε\begin{align*} f(a + \varepsilon) \cdot g(a + \varepsilon) &= (f(a) + f'(a)\varepsilon)(g(a) + g'(a)\varepsilon) \\ &= f(a)g(a) + (f'(a)g(a) + f(a)g'(a))\varepsilon \end{align*}f(a+ε)⋅g(a+ε)​=(f(a)+f′(a)ε)(g(a)+g′(a)ε)=f(a)g(a)+(f′(a)g(a)+f(a)g′(a))ε​

and the chain rule:

f(g(a+ε))=f(g(a)+g′(a)ε)=f(g(a))+f′(g(a))g′(a)ε\begin{align*} f(g(a + \varepsilon)) &= f(g(a) + g'(a)\varepsilon) \\ &= f(g(a)) + f'(g(a))g'(a)\varepsilon \end{align*}f(g(a+ε))​=f(g(a)+g′(a)ε)=f(g(a))+f′(g(a))g′(a)ε​

all work out as expected. That is, the “ε\varepsilonε part”—which we'll call the differential or tangential part—is the derivative of the sum, product, or composition of functions.

But Why?

The discussion above doesn't really get to the heart of the matter, though. This burning question is really something more along the lines of: how does using special numbers allow us to differentiate functions for free? Along the same lines, does this reveal anything about differentiation?

This seems to imply that differentiation is a really easy problem, specifically that it's no more difficult than adding and multiplying things.
